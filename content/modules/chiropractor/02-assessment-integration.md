# Assessment Integration

Integrating DEXA body composition data into chiropractic assessment enhances diagnostic accuracy and treatment planning. Body composition metrics provide objective context for musculoskeletal complaints, reveal contributing factors that clinical examination alone may not identify, and create measurable benchmarks for tracking rehabilitation progress.

## Adding Composition to Your Assessment

![Chiropractic assessment workflow](/content/assets/diagrams/assessment-workflow.png)

### Initial Evaluation

When reviewing a patient's DEXA results during initial evaluation, a systematic approach ensures that clinically relevant findings are not overlooked. Begin by noting overall composition—body fat percentage and total lean mass establish the patient's general condition. Assess regional balance by examining arm and leg asymmetries, which may correlate with compensation patterns or injury sequelae. Evaluate trunk composition, as core muscle mass is directly relevant to spinal stability and loading tolerance. Check VAT levels, which carry both metabolic and postural implications—elevated visceral fat shifts the center of gravity anteriorly, increasing mechanical demands on the lumbar spine.

### Correlating Findings

Connecting DEXA data to clinical presentation transforms body composition numbers into actionable diagnostic insights. The following correlations are commonly observed in chiropractic practice.

| DEXA Finding | Clinical Correlation |
|--------------|---------------------|
| Low trunk lean mass | Core instability, chronic LBP |
| Leg asymmetry >10% | Gait abnormality, compensation |
| High VAT + central fat | Increased lumbar lordosis, facet loading |
| Low overall lean mass | Generalized deconditioning |

These correlations provide a framework for connecting objective data to subjective complaints and clinical findings. When DEXA data aligns with the clinical picture, it strengthens diagnostic confidence. When it diverges, the discrepancy itself is informative and may point toward factors not yet considered.

## Case Integration Examples

### Case 1: Chronic Low Back Pain

**DEXA findings:** Body fat at 34 percent, VAT at 156 cm², low trunk lean mass, and right leg lean mass exceeding left leg by 8 percent.

**Integration:** The elevated VAT contributes to anterior weight shift and increased mechanical loading on the lumbar spine. Low core muscle mass reduces spinal stability, creating a predisposition to facet joint irritation and disc loading. The 8 percent leg asymmetry suggests a guarding or compensation pattern, likely with reduced loading of the left lower extremity. Treatment planning should include core strengthening as a priority, with specific attention to addressing the left leg weakness through unilateral exercises and progressive loading.

### Case 2: Shoulder Pain

**DEXA findings:** Right arm lean mass 12 percent lower than left, overall adequate lean mass, and low body fat consistent with an athletic patient.

**Integration:** The asymmetry in a right-handed patient is opposite the expected pattern—right-dominant individuals typically have slightly greater right arm lean mass. This finding suggests injury, deconditioning from disuse, or a condition limiting right upper extremity use. Rehabilitation should specifically address right upper extremity strengthening, and the DEXA data provides a quantitative baseline against which recovery can be measured.

## Progress Monitoring

Serial DEXA scans provide objective benchmarks for tracking rehabilitation outcomes and overall health improvement across the treatment course.

### Rehabilitation Success

During rehabilitation, DEXA monitors the metrics most relevant to functional recovery: muscle mass improvements in targeted regions, progressive restoration of limb symmetry, and core strength development reflected in trunk lean mass. These measurements complement subjective pain scales and functional assessments by providing objective evidence that structural changes are occurring alongside symptomatic improvement.

### Overall Health

Beyond the specific complaint, DEXA tracks broader health markers that influence musculoskeletal outcomes: overall body composition improvements, VAT reduction with lifestyle changes, and lean mass preservation or gain. Patients who improve their overall body composition often experience better treatment outcomes and more sustained relief from musculoskeletal complaints.

:::tip
Schedule follow-up scans to coincide with treatment plan milestones. This provides objective data for both assessment and patient motivation.
:::

## Communicating Findings

When discussing DEXA results with patients, effective communication follows a three-step framework. First, **connect to their complaint**: "Your core muscle mass is lower than expected, which may be contributing to your back pain stability issues." This makes the data personally relevant. Second, **make it actionable**: "The exercises I'm prescribing specifically target improving this muscle balance." This transforms data into a concrete plan. Third, **set measurable goals**: "We'll repeat the scan in 3-4 months to see if the strengthening work is showing results." This creates accountability and gives patients something to work toward.

## Documentation

Clinical documentation should include relevant DEXA metrics, how those findings inform the diagnosis, any treatment modifications based on body composition data, and specific goals for follow-up scans. This creates a clear record connecting objective data to clinical decision-making and demonstrates evidence-based practice.
